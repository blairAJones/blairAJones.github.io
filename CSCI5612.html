Understanding Online Shopping Trends using ML

In 2024, eBay.com had $8.6Bn marketplace revenue and Amazon.com had $530Bn revenue. The US Census Bureau of the Department of Commerce estimates US online retail sales of over $300Bn for just the first quarter of 2025 alone. Per the Federal Reserve Bank of St. Louis, this represents 16.2% of total retail sales, a percentage that has been steadily climbing since 2000, with a larger jump in 2020. Online retail sites are teeming with data, both supplier and customer generated. The data on these sites can be harnessed to help both buyers and sellers understand market forces, without having to fully rely on the retailer's algorithm. Both eBay and Amazon utilize sophisticated models on their own data for example; however, one needs to consider the motivation of the corporation, compared with the buyers and sellers. Online shopping is a significant source of revenue for some of the world's largest corporations and is a near daily habit for millions of people. Resources that improve transparency in retail make for more efficient markets and a more empowered consumer.

eBay maintains an API service for developer accounts which can return data on current listings for user-defined browsing categories. Unfortunately, the API service which tracked final sales was deprecated in 2024. However, it is believed that current listings can tell a story on market prices for select items, and various quantitative and qualitative features can be used to model price prediction and the flagging of competitively priced or overpriced items.

Amazon listings were obtained from a 3rd party service called Rainforest API. This service allows the user to obtain current listings based on search term or drill-down specifically on items based on asin (Amazon.com specific ID).

To start, the eBay API was utilized for searching two different types of products: first, iPhone 16, which is a recent and popular consumer good that has many listings and some differentiation (model type, storage, etc.). Then, another eBay search was completed on soccer jerseys, which are highly specific, with some seen as “collectible”. This specific search was of interest because of the upcoming World Cup in 2026 (both club and country jerseys appeared in this search).

On Amazon, there were two searches performed: Microwaves, a fairly homogeneous product with potential large price impacts from recent tariff actions, and Lego, a popular product that can have a “style” factor. Depending on if data can be made available from another 3rd party service which tracks historical Amazon prices, additional analysis on price increases in the last year would be compelling.

The data cleaning process continues throughout the project as important information is embedded within product titles on these websites; especially for eBay in which the seller is able to "sell" their product with additional details not found in a specific field. For example, iPhone model numbers and carrier restrictions were found in the iPhone titles and specific team and country information were found in the soccer jerseys data. For the Amazon data, microwave sizes and Lego styles / age recommendations are found in the microwaves and Lego data, respectively. For the eBay data, title length (by characters) and number of additional images posted were considered new calculated fields as features to assess listing quality.

As more models are tested, additional findings are noted. Pricing tier classification using Random Forest models worked well on almost all data sets, and noted different levels of feature importance. This indicates that there are inherent differences in product pricing strategies. For example, from the RF models on the iPhones and Soccer data, respectively, the top feature importance for pricing tiers was model number (product differentiation) for iPhones and seller feedback score (seller differentiation) for Soccer jerseys. For Microwaves and Lego products, cubic feet (product differentiation) and ratings totals (product popularity) were the top important features, respectively.

Working through additional classification models like support vector machines revealed the power of hyperparameter tuning. Many combinations of parameters were trained and validated with all data sets returning different results. In addition, post-hoc analysis like calibration was completed to further refine models (in this case, decision thresholds) based on probabilities. Changing scoring metrics in grid search tuning showed that there is a delicate balance to parameters in relation to model performance as one slight change can meaningfully change test results. As this data is not the type that would suffer greatly from poor recall (as would medical testing or fraud), one needs to consider the balance of recall and the other metrics like accuracy and precision.

The project plans to work with the available retail data and answer the following questions:

  • Can item prices be accurately predicted based on current listings, including information like days-on-hand, markdowns, seller/product ratings, and recent sales (Amazon)?
  • Which features are most significant in model predictions?
  • Do machine learning models vary based on product type? For example, iPhone 16 vs. soccer jerseys, where there is some differentiation with phone model types but lots of similar listings, compared with soccer jerseys which are highly specific.
  • Do prices follow a normal distribution, or would a log transformation be prudent? Or possibly modeling based on another distributi on, like using Poission for recent sales counts, for example.
  • Can a machine learning model help determine which items or listings are competitively priced or overpriced, compared to others?
  • On eBay, do longer titles and more pictures indicate a higher quality listing?
  • Are there differences in feature significance with seller ratings on eBay compared with product ratings on Amazon?
  • Will tree-based models perform better on this data given the number of qualitative features?
  • How could a recommender system and / or sentiment analysis of reviews be incorporated into future versions of the model?
  • (Likely for future analysis) Have the announcements for recent tariffs caused preemptive or necessary increases in prices for home goods (e.g. microwaves)? Answering this would require gaining access to another API service which tracks historical Amazon prices.
